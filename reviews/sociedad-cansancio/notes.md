# La sociedad del cansancio (4ª Ed.)

**Reference**: Byung-Chul Han. La sociedad del cansancio (4ª Ed.). Edición Especial. 2016.

## Prometeo extenuado · Prólogo ·

Prometheus figures the psychic structure of the contemporary performance subject. The subject imagines itself free, yet remains chained by the demand to perform. The eagle that devours Prometheus's liver becomes the subject's own *alter ego*: the force that attacks the subject is not external domination but a relation of the subject to itself.

Self-exploitation turns the subject into both attacker and attacked. The regenerated liver marks an endless cycle of injury, renewal, and renewed injury. Its pain becomes fatigue, and Prometheus becomes the archetype of the society of tiredness.

Kafka's version of the myth gives tiredness a different meaning. The gods tire, the eagles tire, and the wound closes from tiredness. *Healing fatigue* differs from irritated exhaustion produced by excessive self-assertion. A lenitive tiredness arises when the ego is set down cordially rather than intensified.

- **Más allá de la sociedad disciplinaria**: Anticipates the performance subject as self-exploiting rather than externally coerced.
- **Sociedad del cansancio**: Anticipates the distinction between destructive exhaustion and a healing, ego-releasing tiredness.

## La violencia neuronal

Every epoch has characteristic illnesses. The early twenty-first century is not defined by bacterial or viral disease but by neuronal illnesses such as depression, ADHD, borderline personality disorder, and burnout. These are not infections caused by external negativity; they are infarcts caused by an *excess of positivity*.

The immunological paradigm belongs to an earlier age organized around sharp distinctions between inside and outside, friend and enemy, self and other. Its logic is defensive and military. It rejects alterity as such, even when the stranger is not hostile.

The present weakens alterity and replaces it with difference. Difference no longer provokes an immunological reaction. Exoticism becomes consumable, and the tourist or consumer no longer functions as an immunological subject.

The central distinction is between two modes of rejection:

- **Immunological rejection**: A reaction against negativity, alterity, and the foreign, independent of quantity.
- **Non-immunological rejection**: A digestive and neuronal response to excess sameness, overproduction, overperformance, and overcommunication.

The violence of positivity is systemic and immanent. It does not come from an enemy, a virus, or an outside. It saturates rather than deprives, exhausts rather than excludes, and therefore remains less immediately visible. Depression, ADHD, and bipolar disorder point to an overload of the same rather than to an alien hostile force.

## Más allá de la sociedad disciplinaria

The contemporary world is no longer Foucault's disciplinary society of hospitals, asylums, prisons, barracks, and factories. It has become a performance society of gyms, offices, banks, airports, shopping centers, and genetic laboratories. Its inhabitants are not obedient subjects but performance subjects, entrepreneurs of themselves.

The disciplinary society operates through negativity. Its modal verb is *must not*, and its institutions generate criminals and the mad. The performance society operates through positivity. Its modal verb is *can*, and its preferred forms are project, initiative, and motivation. It generates the depressed and the failed.

This transition is not a total rupture. Both societies preserve the social drive to maximize production. Discipline reaches a productivity limit, so the negative command gives way to the more efficient positive imperative. The performance subject remains disciplined, but the command to perform appears as freedom.

Depression is not adequately explained as failure to become oneself or as the burden of self-ownership alone. It is also produced by weakened bonds, social atomization, systemic violence, and the pressure to perform. Burnout is not merely an exhausted ego; it is a burned-out soul under the new commandment of late-modern labor.

The performance subject is both victim and perpetrator. External domination disappears, but freedom becomes coercive self-obligation. Autoexploitation is more efficient than exploitation by others because it carries the feeling of freedom.

## Aburrimiento profundo

The excess of positivity also appears as an excess of stimuli, information, and impulses. Attention fragments and disperses. Multitasking is not a civilizational advance but a regression toward the vigilance of animals in the wild, where attention must constantly be distributed among threats, prey, offspring, and surroundings.

Human culture depends on intense contemplative attention. Philosophy and other cultural achievements require an environment in which attention can deepen. *Hyperattention* replaces this depth with rapid shifts among tasks, sources, and processes. It cannot tolerate boredom, and therefore cannot sustain the deep boredom needed for creative experience.

Deep boredom is spiritually analogous to bodily relaxation. Stress only reproduces and accelerates what already exists. Benjamin's image of boredom as the bird that hatches the egg of experience links creativity to pauses, nests of time, and listening. The loss of relaxation destroys the capacity for attentive listening and replaces communities of listeners with communities of active subjects.

The ability to endure boredom allows movement to change form. Mere acceleration turns walking into running, but deep boredom can transform walking into dance. Dance exceeds the straight line of performance.

Contemplation is not limited to immutable being. It perceives the fleeting, the subtle, and the durable. Hyperactivity cannot perceive duration. A contemplative gaze empties the self and lets things appear.

## Vita activa

Hannah Arendt rehabilitates the *vita activa* against the traditional priority of the *vita contemplativa*. Action becomes heroic because it is tied to birth, beginning, and the miraculous capacity to initiate something new. Modern labor society, in Arendt's account, degrades human beings into *animal laborans* and reduces action and production to labor.

That diagnosis does not fit late-modern performance society. The contemporary *animal laborans* does not dissolve passively into the anonymous life of the species. It is individualized, ego-saturated, hyperactive, and hyperneurotic. It is anything but animal in the sense of serene immersion in life.

The loss of faith, reality, and narrative makes life radically fleeting. Without durable meaning or techniques for assimilating death, life becomes *naked life*. Work becomes naked activity, and health becomes absolute because no horizon beyond survival remains.

The logic of *homo sacer* extends beyond excluded figures. In performance society, everyone is reduced to naked life, yet not as someone who can simply be killed. The late-modern subject resembles a living dead person: preserved at all costs, functionally alive, and spiritually depleted.

The labor society of performance is coercive. Each person carries a labor camp within, as both prisoner and guard, victim and perpetrator. Arendt's final appeal to thought unintentionally gestures back toward contemplation, because the loss of contemplative capacity belongs to the causes of modern nervousness and hysteria.

## Pedagogía de la mirada

The *vita contemplativa* requires a pedagogy of the gaze. Nietzsche's tasks of learning to see, think, speak, and write begin with the eye. Learning to see means accustoming the eye to calm, patience, and waiting until things arrive. Spirituality begins with the capacity not to react immediately to every stimulus.

Contemplation is not passive acceptance. It is sovereign resistance to the avalanche of impulses. Hyperactivity becomes hyperpassivity when it leaves the subject at the mercy of every stimulus. More activity does not mean more freedom.

Action requires interruption. Without inhibition and formation, action dissolves into frantic reaction and discharge. A world without intervals, interruptions, and in-between times reduces action to work. Hesitation and delay can preserve action from mechanical activity.

Furor differs from irritation. Irritation responds to particular annoyances and cannot produce radical change. Furor negates the total situation and interrupts the present, allowing something new to begin. A fully positivized world weakens exception, anguish, and sadness because these depend on negativity.

Power has two forms:

- **Positive power**: The capacity to do something.
- **Negative power**: The capacity not to do, not to perceive, or to say no.

Negative power is not impotence. It enables reflection, spirituality, and free action. Meditation seeks the active negativity of non-doing. Hyperactivity absolutizes positive power and thereby becomes an extremely passive form of doing.

## El caso Bartleby

Melville's “Bartleby” admits a pathological reading. Wall Street appears as an inhuman labor world whose inhabitants have become laboring animals. The office is lifeless, gloomy, and enclosed. Turkey and Nippers display neurotic agitation, psychosomatic disturbance, ambition, and irritability, while Bartleby appears silent, hieratic, apathetic, and neurasthenic.

Bartleby's formula, “I would prefer not to,” does not express the negative power of non-doing or a spiritual instinct of inhibition. It expresses abulia and apathy, and Bartleby dies from this condition.

The story still belongs to disciplinary society. Walls, partitions, blind walls, and the prison called the Tombs dominate its topology. Bartleby remains an obedient subject rather than a late-modern performance subject. He does not suffer from an excess of possibilities, fear of failure, self-reproach, or the imperative to become himself. Copying leaves no space for initiative.

Agamben's ontotheological reading elevates Bartleby into a figure of pure potentiality, but this ignores pathology, narrative details, and the current structural change of the psyche. Bartleby is worldless, unrelated, blank through lack of sense rather than divine potential.

The dead-letter office links Bartleby to messages of life rushing toward death. The small patch of grass in the prison does not create messianic hope; it intensifies the hopelessness of the realm of death. Bartleby's story is not decreation but *exhaustion*.

## Sociedad del cansancio

Performance society becomes a society of doping. “Neural enhancement” presents itself as a positive alternative to “brain doping,” and the whole person becomes a performance machine required to function smoothly and maximize output. Doping is only a consequence of a deeper reduction of vitality to functions and measurable performance.

The other side of this development is excessive tiredness and exhaustion. These states arise in a world poor in negativity and overloaded with positivity. Excess performance produces an infarct of the soul.

Two forms of tiredness must be distinguished:

- **Separating tiredness**: Isolates individuals, destroys speech, narrows vision to the ego, and becomes violent by deforming the other.
- **Fundamental tiredness**: Reduces the ego, opens the subject to the world, restores duality, and creates a shared interval.

Handke's fundamental tiredness is eloquent, seeing, and reconciling. It creates trust in the world and makes mutual contact possible. It permits lingering, form, rhythm, and a different attention capable of perceiving duration and repose.

Inspired tiredness is not incapacity. It is a faculty that inspires non-doing. It belongs to negative power rather than exhausted positive power. Sabbath, originally cessation, is a day without purpose, a workless interlude, and a time for play. A society shaped by this shared, peaceful fatigue would be a society of tiredness in a healing sense.

- **Prometeo extenuado · Prólogo ·**: Returns to the idea of tiredness that closes wounds rather than intensifies exhaustion.

## Sociedad del burnout

Freud's psychic apparatus belongs to a disciplinary society of repression, prohibitions, and commands. The Freudian subject is governed by negation, repression, and fear of transgression. The late-modern performance subject is different: it is a subject of affirmation, defined by *can* rather than *must*.

Kant's moral subject and Freud's subject both involve inner division, judgment, duty, and an other within the self. The performance subject appears liberated from external command and expects pleasure from work, but this liberation produces new coercions.

The loss of relation to the other creates a crisis of gratification. Recognition requires an other or a third party. Narcissism dissolves the boundary between self and other, replacing experience with self-experience. The subject never reaches a final point of repose because the imperative of performance demands constant increase. Burnout names the collapse produced by competition with oneself.

Current psychic illnesses are not modeled by repression:

- **Hysteria**: Belongs to disciplinary negativity, repression, and characteristic form.
- **Depression and burnout**: Belong to excess positivity, formlessness, inability to say no, and self-directed exhaustion.
- **ADHD**: Belongs to the same field of excessive positivity rather than hidden conflict.

Digital communication further weakens alterity by letting the ego encounter mostly itself. Depression lacks object, orientation, and stable bonds. It differs from mourning and melancholy because it is not organized around a lost object.

The performance subject projects itself into an unreachable ego ideal. The project becomes a projectile turned against the subject. The subject is simultaneously sovereign and *homo sacer* of itself, captured by the spell of performance, autoexploitation, survival, and health as a new empty divinity.

### Relationships With Other Chapters

- **La violencia neuronal**: Expands the diagnosis of positivity into a psychoanalytic, economic, and political account of burnout.

## El tiempo sublime

The present is a time without festivity. A feast has a distinctive temporality: it is celebrated, not traversed as a sequence of moments. Festive time does not pass in the ordinary way. It is a *sublime time*, an image of eternity measured by lingering and staying.

Feast and art share this temporal structure. Both suspend mere succession and open a space in which life becomes intense. Religious feast, sacrifice, song, dance, and play establish relation to the divine. Work and production do not place human beings among gods; the absolutization of work destroys sublime time.

Slowing down is insufficient. The crisis of time requires another form of life and another narrative, not merely acceleration or deceleration. The sacred threshold separating feast from profane daily time has disappeared, and even pauses are absorbed into labor time as recovery for further functioning.

Performance society carries a portable labor camp. Each person is prisoner and guard, victim and perpetrator, master and servant. Autoexploitation feels like freedom and begins with euphoria, but ends in collapse. Freedom of ability produces unlimited coercion because ability has no upper limit.

Life is reduced to survival, health, optimization, and market value. Beauty, politics, and free life require forms not governed by necessity and utility. Profanation assigns things a freer use, as when children turn money into a toy. Work, production, capital, and labor time need profanation into play and festivity.

Art originally belonged to feast and cult, but market and exhibition value displace cult value. Hypercapitalism turns persons, relations, and lifetime into commercial value. The world becomes a transparent department store full of commodities but poor in world, silence, mystery, sacredness, and astonishment.

### Relationships With Other Chapters

- **Sociedad del cansancio**: Extends non-doing, interlude, and play into a broader account of festive time.
- **Sociedad del burnout**: Returns to autoexploitation, survival, and health as symptoms of performance society.

## Possible Derived Thematic Notes

- **Negativity and Positivity in Late Modernity**: The shift from prohibition, alterity, and repression to excess, sameness, and affirmation.
- **Autoexploitation and Coercive Freedom**: The subject as entrepreneur, victim, perpetrator, and project turned against itself.
- **Contemplation, Attention, and Deep Boredom**: The cultural role of non-reaction, delay, listening, and intense perception.
- **Tiredness as Pathology and Healing**: The contrast between isolating exhaustion and shared, world-opening fatigue.
- **Survival, Health, and Naked Life**: The reduction of life to biological persistence, functioning, and optimization.
- **Festivity, Play, and Profanation**: The recovery of sublime time against labor time, market value, and total production.
